A long time ago, in a supply chain far, far away...
The IRAN WAR has thrown galactic trade into chaos. The Strait of Hormuz stands CLOSED. Qatari and UAE energy exports have gone dark. Maersk and Hapag-Lloyd have fled the Middle Eastern theater entirely.
Ports around the Indian Ocean overflow with stranded cargo. A shipment of hair clips and playdough drifts helplessly off Karachi, costing its owner $200 a day in fees. More than 100 vessels are trapped in the Gulf. The Empire of Global Trade trembles.
Meanwhile, in the warehouse sector, a rebellion stirs. Big-box vacancies hit multi-year highs. Cold-storage rates collapse. The Rebel Alliance of Third-Party Logistics scrambles — DHL marshals 10 new outposts, and FedEx raises its flag over a battered UPS.
And then — from out of the chaos — a beacon of hope. With 1.3 seconds remaining, UConn guard Braylon Mullins launches a 35-foot buzzer-beater over Duke. Huskies 71, Blue Devils 70.
200,000 fans flood team stores demanding the Mullins #24 jersey. Manufacturers, wholesalers, 3PLs, and retailers across the value network strain under the surge. Someone must move those $50 jerseys through the channel — fast, efficiently, and at a profit.
Choose your distribution intensity, your push or pull strategy, your channel intermediary, your inventory level, and your transportation mode. The value chain awaits.

1 · Distribution Intensity LO 12.5
Distribution intensity determines how many outlets carry your product. Intensive = maximum exposure (convenience/event goods). Selective = chosen retailers. Exclusive = prestige, limited access. Your choice directly affects how many fans can find the jersey.
2 · Marketing Strategy: Push vs. Pull LO 12.5
Push strategy directs spend toward channel intermediaries — slotting allowances, trade deals — to get product on shelves. Pull strategy invests in end-consumer marketing (social media, influencers) to generate demand that pulls product through the channel.
3 · Channel Intermediary / Supplier LO 12.2/12.3
Intermediaries link producers to consumers and perform physical distribution, transaction, and facilitating functions. A 3PL lets your firm focus on core business by outsourcing fulfillment. A wholesaler buys in bulk and resells — low cost, higher disruption risk.
4 · Inventory Management LO 12.6
JIT (just-in-time) minimizes holding costs but risks stockouts when demand spikes. Safety stock buffers against uncertainty but raises warehousing costs. Jerseys sell for $50 each.
